Attention control development in early childhood

Frontiers in Psychology, 2022

 

This is my first experimental paper and the one that I am most proud of. Why? Because I mostly managed the whole project on my own (from experimental design to ethics committee submission, participant recruitment, data collection, statistical analysis and report). The project was also hit by the COVID-19 lockdown, leading to a reduction in our sample and rethinking the originally planned analyses.

My goal was to get a deeper understanding of attention control development in early childhood. It is known that from 2 years of age, the executive attention network starts gaining more weight over attentional control to the detriment of the orienting network. Based on this, my supervisor and I aimed to explore the transition in attentional control between both networks and to identify factors that contribute to predicting attention control development.

I recruited 150 toddlers and young children between the ages of 2 and 4 years from several kindergartens and primary schools in the city of Granada (Spain). The participants were assigned to 5 different age groups (2, 2.5, 3, 3.5, and 4 years). I programmed the Visual Sequence Learning task (Clohessy et al., 2001 - ActaPsy) to be used with eye-tracking (SMI RED250Mobile) to measure exogenously controlled attention, voluntary controlled attention and executive attention.

Through hierarchical regression models, I was able to test the contribution of age, temperamental factors and home chaos to the prediction of visual attention control. In general, age predicted increases in endogenous and executive attention control, but decrements in exogenous attention. Surprisingly, home chaos predicted a higher executive attention later in development.

Influence of the environment on the early development of attentional control

Infant Behaviour and Development, 2023

 

My second paper used data from the BEXAT longitudinal study that my team and I conducted with infants for 4 years. In this article, I present results concerning visual attention disengagement development from 6 to 16-18 months of age using eye-tracking (Eyelink 1000 Plus). It was a team effort, from participant recruitment, to   programming the experimental tasks, data collection and statistical analysis. The main challenge of this project was to deal with missing data.

The main goal was to characterize the development of visual attention disengagement (Gap-overlap task) from infancy to toddlerhood and to test which factors related to the individual, but also environmental factors of the rearing environment contribute to predicting attentional disengagement at later ages. We recruited 260 families from the city of Granada that participated in the study.

Through linear mixed models (LMMs) hierarchical regressions, I was able to find that with age infants gain control over their voluntary control of visual orientation, showing a higher control in contexts where visual disengagement is facilitated. However, I found no longitudinal changes in visual disengagement in contexts of visual competition. Interestingly, infants' socioeconomic background and home chaos predicted a lower failure to disengage attention in toddlerhood.

Predicting effortful control at 3 years of age

Children, 2023

 

My third paper as first author was a collaboration with Dr. Mariel Musso to identify which attentional factors and those of the rearing environment in infancy, contributed to predict effortful control during early childhood. For this, I curated all the attentional measures derived from the eye-tracking tasks that I programmed for the study, as well as some questionnaire measures.

Mariel used Neural Networks with the IBM SPSS Neural Networks module to identify which early factors contributed the most to the prediction of effortful control. The higher predictive weights were found for factors related to infants' socioeconomic background and early attentional control at 6 months of age.

Gender inclusive language cretes identity threat and protection againts stereotype threat for women

Preprint

 

The data collection of the data presented in this preprint started during my Master's thesis. At that time, I got interested in gender studies. Working alongside my supervisor at that time, Dr. Soledad de Lemus, I designed a study to explore the effects of gender exclusive (using masculine to refer to both male and female subjects) and gender-inclusive language (use masculine and feminine forms in language), when women experience or do not, stereotype threat (an effect in which a negative stereotype about the person's social group is activated and is relevant to the current context). This study was designed from scratch and needed to go through an ethics committee review.

In this preprint, I used an Integrative Data Analysis (IDA) to pool the data from the original study and a replication that I conducted a year later with the help of the co-authors. The total sample size was 350 women, and I collected measures of cognitive performance, motivation, self-efficacy and feelings of exclusion.

Results revealed that using gender-exclusive language led to more anxiety related to cognitive performance than gender-inclusive language, independently of the threat condition. Moreover, gender-inclusive language elicited lower levels of exclusion.
